Local Info

On foot

Canadian Clay and Glass Gallery

Contemporary ceramic and glass art in a compact, walkable museum.

By foot: 20–30 min

The Canadian Clay and Glass Gallery is a small, focused museum with frequently changing exhibitions in ceramics, glass, and related craft traditions. It is an easy walk from the university area and pairs well with a stroll through nearby parks and cafés. If you have an hour between sessions, it is an unusually high-quality cultural stop for its size.

Photo: “C2G2” (Wikimedia Commons).

Short ride within Waterloo Region

St. Jacobs Farmers’ Market and Village

A classic Ontario market day with Mennonite-country flavour.

By car: 15–20 min; by public transport: 40–60 min

St. Jacobs is the region’s best-known market destination, combining fresh food, local baking, and small artisans in a lively atmosphere. The surrounding village has walkable shops and an old-Canada streetscape that visitors often find charming and photogenic. It is a good half-day outing, especially on market days, and is easy to combine with other nearby stops.

Photo: “St Jacobs Farmers Market 2021” (Wikimedia Commons).

West Montrose Covered Bridge

The “kissing bridge” over the Grand River, built in the 1880s.

By car: 25–30 min; taxi/uber: about C$45–C$70 one way

This is one of the most iconic historic structures in the region: a long, red wooden covered bridge spanning the Grand River. The area is quiet and scenic, making it a pleasant “short escape” from the city for photos and a riverside walk. If you enjoy engineering history and vernacular architecture, it is a memorable local landmark.

Photo: “The West Montrose Covered Bridge (50399542651).jpg” (Wikimedia Commons, CC0).

Elora Gorge

Limestone cliffs and river scenery in one of Ontario’s best small gorges.

By car: 35–45 min; taxi/uber: about C$55–C$85 one way

Elora Gorge is a dramatic stretch of the Grand River carved into limestone, with trails, viewpoints, and (in season) water activities. The nearby village of Elora adds cafés, galleries, and a compact historic main street, making the outing feel complete. It works well as a half-day trip for visitors who want nature without committing to a long drive.

Photo: “Elora gorge.jpg” (Wikimedia Commons, CC BY-SA).

Full-day trips

Stratford Festival and Stratford

World-class theatre in a picturesque small city.

By car: 45–60 min

Stratford is famous for its theatre festival, with performances ranging from Shakespeare to contemporary plays and musicals. The city itself is attractive and walkable, with river paths, cafés, and good dining, so it is enjoyable even if you do not attend an evening show. Many conference visitors treat Stratford as the signature cultural day trip from Waterloo.

Photo: “Festival theatre, Stratford.jpg” (Wikimedia Commons).

Niagara Falls and Niagara-on-the-Lake

Iconic waterfalls plus a historic town with theatres and shops.

By car: 1 h 20–1 h 45; by public transport: 2 h 15–3 h

Niagara Falls is one of North America’s most dramatic natural sights, with viewpoints, boat trips, and easy riverside walking routes. Nearby Niagara-on-the-Lake offers a contrasting pace: a historic main street, lake views, and cultural events that make it feel like a different world from the tourist strip. Many visitors combine them in one long day to get both the spectacle and the charm.

Photo: “Niagara Falls - ON - Niagarafälle.jpg” (Wikimedia Commons).

Niagara wineries

Vineyards, tastings, and Canada’s best-known cool-climate wine region.

By car: 1 h 30–2 h

The Niagara Peninsula is the centre of Ontario wine, with vineyards and tasting rooms spread across an easy-to-navigate rural landscape. Many wineries offer short tastings that fit naturally around Niagara Falls or Niagara-on-the-Lake, and the region is especially known for ice wine and crisp whites. If you enjoy food culture, this is a rewarding way to see the agricultural side of southern Ontario.

Photo: “Grapevines in Niagara-on-the-Lake.jpg” (Wikimedia Commons, CC BY).

Woodland Cultural Centre

An important museum and education centre for Haudenosaunee history and culture.

By car: 50–65 min

The Woodland Cultural Centre offers exhibitions and programming that present Haudenosaunee culture, history, and contemporary life in an interpretive museum setting. The site includes the former Mohawk Institute Residential School building, which adds a sober and historically significant dimension to the visit. For visitors interested in Canadian history beyond the usual tourist circuit, this is one of the most meaningful regional destinations.

Photo: “MohawkInstitute.jpg” (Wikimedia Commons, CC BY-SA).

Ska-Nah-Doht Village and Museum

Reconstructed longhouse village and archaeology-focused Indigenous heritage site.

By car: 1 h 15–1 h 35

Ska-Nah-Doht presents an archaeological and cultural window into an earlier Iroquoian settlement, with reconstructions and interpretation geared to understanding everyday life. It is a distinctive, hands-on kind of historical outing that complements the more museum-like experience at the Woodland Cultural Centre. If you are looking for something unique to southwestern Ontario, this is a strong choice.

Photo: “Turnoff to Ska-Nah-Doht Iroquoian Village, Delaware, Ontario (21840568645).jpg” (Wikimedia Commons, CC BY-SA).

Rock Glen Conservation Area

Waterfalls, fossil hunting, and forest trails in a compact park.

By car: 1 h 20–1 h 45

Rock Glen is known for a picturesque waterfall and for exposed rock layers where visitors often search for small fossils. The trails are short but satisfying, and the outing feels like a miniature geology field trip in the woods. It is especially appealing for conference participants who enjoy natural history and want an experience that is uncommon in typical city tourism.

Photo: “Rock Glen Falls.jpg” (Wikimedia Commons).

Grand Bend

Lake Huron beach town with sunsets and a classic boardwalk feel.

By car: 1 h 20–1 h 45

Grand Bend is a popular summer destination on Lake Huron, known for a wide sandy beach and long sunset views over open water. Even outside peak season, the shoreline and pier area make for a relaxing change of pace from the city. It is a good option for visitors who want “a Great Lakes beach day” without committing to a multi-day trip.

Photo: “Grand Bend 3.JPG” (Wikimedia Commons).

Long day or overnight trips

Point Pelee National Park

The southernmost point of mainland Canada, famous for bird migration.

By car: 3 h 00–3 h 45

Point Pelee is a striking sandspit that extends into Lake Erie, creating a distinctive landscape and a renowned birding destination during migration seasons. The park has easy trails and boardwalks through marsh and forest, so it works for both casual walkers and serious naturalists. It is a long drive, but the setting is unlike anything near Waterloo and can be a highlight for visitors who love ecology.

Photo: “Point Pelee National Park- The Tip of Canada- Ontario.jpg” (Wikimedia Commons, CC BY-SA).

Bruce Peninsula National Park and the Grotto area

Turquoise water, rugged limestone cliffs, and iconic Georgian Bay scenery.

By car: 2 h 45–3 h 30

The Bruce Peninsula offers some of Ontario’s most dramatic shoreline, with cliffs, clear water, and forest trails leading to viewpoints near the Grotto. The landscape feels distinctly different from southern Ontario farmland and cities, and the photos can look almost Mediterranean on a bright day. Because it is popular, planning ahead is helpful, and many visitors prefer to make it an overnight trip.

Photo: “Bruce Peninsula National Park cliffs.jpg” (Wikimedia Commons, CC BY-SA).
